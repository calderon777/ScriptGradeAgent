The model suggests VAT can be a solid but imperfect tool for improving education equity. The welfare analysis shows that, holding the student allocation constant, VAT unambiguously raises total welfare <latex>𝑑𝑊𝑑𝜏>0</latex>                  due to redistribution from higher income families funding public goods, which reminds me of the equity argument in Lecture 9.

While this promoted integration, it may generate deadweight loss through congestion in public schools and the loss of M’s high private valuation <latex>𝑃𝑀</latex>    , which links to the IFS concerns about pressure on middle income households. High income families H, due to their high <latex>𝑃𝐻</latex>,    are more likely to absorb the tax cost and stay private, making the tax progressive overall. Low-income families L unambiguously gain from better funded public schools. 

The model has limitations relevant to real world debate, it assumes perfect rationality, ignoring present bias, where parents might overreact to upfront cost increases and not save enough for the future. It abstracts from Coasean bargaining failures and short run fixed costs that could lead to school closures and job losses.

What's the conclusion? To maximise equity gains while minimising efficiency costs like deadweight loss and middle-income burden, the VAT policy should be complemented with targeted measures. Income linked bursaries could lower the effective <latex>𝜏 </latex>for struggling families, and transitional support for schools could mitigate fixed cost shocks. Successful implementation requires managing these trade-offs.

VAT revenue <latex>𝑇 </latex>   raises public school payoffs <latex>5𝑇𝑥</latex>,      helping low- and middle-income families who stay in public schools. In the model, this can raise total welfare <latex>𝑑𝑊𝑑𝜏>0</latex>              by offsetting inequality coming from different <latex>𝑃𝑖</latex>     values. That fits with our lecture 9’s view of education as a main driver of social mobility. (Green and Kynaston, 2019)

The second video, instead, points to short run adjustment costs that the static model doesn't fully capture, like job losses and school closures. The model hints at this through fixed costs: if schools face high fixed costs, a fall in private enrolment <latex>𝑦 </latex>can push them into losses and force exits, creating disruptions. To analyse this properly, the model would need a dynamic, two stage extension. (Lambert and Atherton, 2025)

1)	In stage 1, schools with fixed cost <latex>𝐾 </latex>   (facilities, staff, teachers) decide whether to operate based on expected <latex>𝑦 </latex>  They only operate if expected fee revenue is <latex>𝐹=𝑦𝑡≥𝐾 </latex>. 

2)	Stage 2: Families choose schools given the set of operating private schools. 

Introducing VAT reduces equilibrium <latex>𝑦 </latex>, potentially pushing some private schools below <latex>𝐹<𝐾 </latex>,           leading to exit, job losses, and reduces choice. (Alpesh Maisuria and Lally, 2024) This would cause a temporary welfare loss and could hurt long run quality through reduced competition, which connects to our lectures on market structures and adjustment costs. 

Furthermore, the second video talks about lobbying and political economy aspects: parents in private schools may advocate for better public education. The model’s moral hazard argument touches on this: expecting state funding might reduce such advocacy. A better model could include school quality investment funded by fees or lobbying. 

In conclusion, while the model effectively captures the equity efficiency trade off, a comprehensive analysis for policymakers must also incorporate dynamic fixed cost effects and political economy feedback to fully weigh the arguments in both videos.
