  The introduction of Value Added Tax (VAT) on private school fees represents a major change in the taxation of independent education. Under the policy, private schools are required to charge VAT at the standard rate on tuition and boarding services that were previously exempt. This marks a clear departure from long-standing tax treatment, under which private education was treated as a VAT-exempt supply, often justified by the charitable status of many institutions.

The principal effect of the reform is an increase in the effective price of private schooling. Although schools may be able to reclaim VAT on some inputs, most are expected to pass on a significant proportion of the tax to parents through higher fees. In addition, the withdrawal of business rates relief for charitable private schools raises operating costs, further intensifying upward pressure on fees. Schools must also meet new administrative requirements, including VAT registration, compliance procedures, and changes to billing systems.

The costs of the policy are likely to fall mainly on households that use private education. Higher fees may reduce affordability, particularly for middle-income families, and may encourage some pupils to transfer into the state education system. This could increase demand for state school places in certain areas and place additional pressure on public resources, especially where capacity is limited. Smaller or financially weaker private schools may also find it difficult to absorb these changes, potentially leading to closures or consolidation.

The benefits of the policy are fiscal and redistributive. Extending VAT broadens the tax base and generates additional public revenue, which can be directed towards state education or other public services. The reform also seeks to remove preferential tax treatment for private schooling, promoting greater equity between families who choose private education and those who depend on the state system. Overall, the trade-offs are significant.
