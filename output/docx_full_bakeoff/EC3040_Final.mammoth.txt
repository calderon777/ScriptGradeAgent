Proposed Grouping: Private school attendance in England, where VAT and business-rate reforms apply, can be compared with Scotland, which abolished business-rate relief earlier. Examining trends before and after the policy change across these regions helps remove common macroeconomic shocks.



3. Practical Implementation

Feasibility of IV: Although IV methods are conceptually attractive, they are difficult to implement in practice, as it is challenging to identify a “clean” instrument that affects tuition fees without influencing enrolment through other channels.

Feasibility of DiD: A DiD approach is more feasible. The ISC Annual Census provides long-run data for England and the devolved regions, allowing researchers to assess whether the predicted 3–7% reduction in private school attendance materialises more strongly in regions subject to higher tax burdens.

Regression Discontinuity (RDD): While less suitable for analysing a broad tax reform, RDD could be applied to specialist provision. Since pupils with Education, Health and Care (EHC) plans are exempt from VAT, comparing pupils just above and below the eligibility threshold would help identify the causal effect of VAT on private school attendance.



Part 4



The theoretical model developed in Part 2, together with the empirical strategies outlined in Part 3, provides a structured framework for evaluating the claims made in the two YouTube videos regarding the introduction of VAT on private schools. In particular, the model helps clarify whether arguments such as “spiralling effects” and the so-called “jealousy tax” are supported by economic incentives and observable behaviour.



Shedding Light on Video Arguments

The payoff functions  and  directly capture the “middle-class squeeze” highlighted in the first video. Variation in intrinsic preferences  implies that families with lower intrinsic preferences for private schooling are more price-sensitive, making Scenario 2 (migration from private to public schools) more likely for middle-income households than for ultra-high-income families with relatively inelastic demand. This aligns closely with the video’s claim that the tax primarily affects marginal private-school users rather than the wealthiest households. The model also incorporates a congestion mechanism through the term S/x, which reflects concerns raised in the videos about the state system “bursting at capacity.” However, the empirical evidence discussed in Part 3 weakens this claim. Demographic trends, including a declining birth rate, suggest that the state sector may have spare capacity. Estimates indicate that up to 700,000 additional pupils could potentially be absorbed without triggering the severe capacity constraints described in the videos.



Arguments Missing from the Model

Despite its usefulness, the model abstracts from several arguments raised in the videos. First, macroeconomic spillovers are not explicitly considered. A reduction in private school fee payments does not necessarily imply a contraction in overall demand, as households may redirect this expenditure toward other goods and services, which would still generate VAT revenue for the state. Second, the model assumes a fixed private school tuition fee t, overlooking supply-side vulnerability. In practice, if private school enrolment y falls below a critical threshold, some schools may become financially unviable and close. Such closures could result in a discrete and non-marginal increase in the number of pupils entering the state sector, rather than the smooth adjustment implied by the model. Finally, labour market responses are not captured. The videos highlight the potential for changes in parental labour supply, such as increased working hours to afford higher fees, as well as the possibility of teacher strikes or staffing pressures in response to changes in funding and enrolment.
