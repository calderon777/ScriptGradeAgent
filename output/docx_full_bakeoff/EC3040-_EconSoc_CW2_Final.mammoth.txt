Re-written answer

The proposal to apply Value Added Tax (VAT) to private school fees would remove the tax exemption currently enjoyed by independent schools and bring private education within the standard VAT framework. In practical terms, private schools would be required to charge VAT on tuition fees, while retaining the ability to reclaim VAT on inputs. Provision funded by local authorities for pupils with specialist needs would continue to be excluded. This reform marks a shift in how private education is treated relative to other consumer services (IFS, 2023).

A key justification for the policy is its potential to strengthen public finances and support state education. Estimates from the Institute for Fiscal Studies suggest that the removal of tax exemptions from private schools could raise substantial revenue, even after accounting for additional costs in the state sector, resulting in a net fiscal gain of around £1.3–£1.5 billion per year (IFS, 2023, pp. 3, 24). From the perspective of the course, this reflects the redistributive role of taxation in addressing high levels of income and wealth inequality in the UK (Lecture 1).

Although introducing VAT would raise the price of private schooling, the evidence indicates that household responses are likely to be limited. Private school attendance has remained relatively stable despite significant increases in fees over time, suggesting that demand is not highly sensitive to price changes (IFS, 2023, pp. 11–12). This implies that only a small proportion of pupils would move into the state sector, keeping additional public spending requirements relatively low.

While VAT is generally classified as a regressive consumption tax (Lecture 2), this classification is less clear-cut in this case. Because private education is predominantly consumed by higher-income households, the effective burden of the tax would fall mainly on those with greater ability to pay. Taken together, the policy illustrates the equity–efficiency trade-off discussed in the lectures: while taxation can distort behaviour, limited responsiveness in this context suggests that the policy may improve equity with only modest efficiency costs.

Is introducing VAT on private schools a good idea?

Whether introducing VAT on private school fees is a good policy depends on the balance between equity gains and efficiency costs. 

The theoretical model developed in this coursework helps to make these trade-offs explicit. In the model, VAT affects families differently depending on their school choice. For any family attending private school, utility includes the term −t(1+τ), implying that an increase in VAT lowers private-school utility by t. By contrast, families attending public school benefit from higher tax revenue. As shown in Part 2.5, public school utility increases with VAT at a rate of 5ty/x, where y is the number of private-school families and x the number of public-school families. This creates a redistribution from private to public school families.

The effect on social welfare can be illustrated by comparing total gains and losses. If there are ‘y’ private-school families, the total welfare loss from VAT is ‘yt’. If there are ‘x’ public-school families, the total welfare gain is ‘x ⋅ 5ty/x= 5ty’. Social welfare therefore increases when the gains to public-school families exceed the losses to private-school families, that is when ‘5ty>yt’, which holds when public funding is sufficiently effective. This condition is more likely to be satisfied in scenario 2, where more families attend public school.

From an equity perspective, VAT on private schools can be justified. High-income families, who place a higher value on private education, are more likely to continue sending their children to private schools and therefore bear most of the tax burden. Low-income families benefit indirectly through improved public education. This reflects the redistributive role of taxation discussed in the lectures.

However, there are also efficiency concerns. If VAT leads to large shifts into public schools without proportional improvements in quality, congestion effects may reduce welfare. This highlights that education is not a pure public good, as discussed in the lectures.

In conclusion, introducing VAT on private schools can be a good idea when the revenue is effectively used to improve public education and redistribution dominates efficiency losses. Otherwise, the policy may reduce overall welfare.

Endogeneity concerns and potential solutions

The regression specified in Question 3 may be affected by endogeneity, meaning that the VAT policy variable could be correlated with unobserved factors in the error term. As discussed repeatedly in the lectures, this makes it difficult to interpret the estimated coefficient as a causal effect.

One potential source of endogeneity is omitted variables. There may be factors that influence both the decision to introduce VAT on private school fees and families’ schooling choices, but which are not directly observed in the data. For example, shifts in political priorities, growing concern about educational inequality, or wider changes to the education system could affect both policy design and private school attendance. If these influences are not fully controlled for, they will bias the estimated effect of the VAT policy.

A second concern is reverse causality, which is emphasised in the lectures when analysing policy outcomes. If private school attendance has been increasing over time and contributing to concerns about segregation or inequality, this may itself prompt policymakers to introduce VAT. In this case, changes in school attendance would influence the policy, rather than the policy influencing school attendance.

These endogeneity issues can be understood using the theoretical model in Part 2, where families’ school choices depend on income, preferences, and redistribution. Many of these factors are difficult to measure empirically, yet they may also shape political support for taxing private education. As a result, the VAT variable may be correlated with unobserved determinants of school choice.

Several approaches could help address these concerns. One option, discussed in the lectures, is a difference-in-differences strategy, which compares changes in private school attendance before and after the introduction of VAT across areas with different exposure to private schooling. This method controls for common time trends and fixed differences between areas, although it relies on the assumption that trends would have been similar in the absence of the policy.

Another possible solution is the use of an instrumental variable that affects the likelihood of VAT being introduced but does not directly affect school choice. However, as highlighted in the lectures, identifying a convincing instrument in this context is likely to be difficult, since most political or fiscal factors influencing the policy may also be related to education outcomes.

Finally, including area and time fixed effects, as done in Question 3, helps reduce bias by controlling for unobserved, time-invariant differences across areas and economy-wide shocks over time. While this does not completely resolve endogeneity, it improves the reliability of the estimates.

In practice, fixed effects and difference-in-differences are relatively feasible with available data, whereas instrumental variable approaches are harder to implement credibly. As stressed throughout the course, careful empirical methods can reduce endogeneity concerns, even if they cannot eliminate them entirely.



Part 4 (15 Marks)

The theoretical framework developed in Part 2 helps clarify several of the arguments raised in the two YouTube videos discussing the introduction of VAT on private school fees. In the model, households decide between private and state schooling by comparing costs and benefits, with VAT increasing the price of private education. This directly reflects a key claim in the videos: that higher fees will discourage private school attendance and lead some families to switch into the state sector.

The empirical approach outlined in Part 3 allows this claim to be evaluated using data. By estimating how private school enrolment changes following the introduction of VAT, we can assess whether the policy has a substantial impact on families’ schooling decisions. This evidence can therefore inform debates in the videos about whether the policy will lead to large behavioural responses or whether demand for private schooling is relatively insensitive to price changes.

At the same time, several points raised in the videos are not fully captured by the baseline model. One commonly mentioned concern is that an inflow of pupils into state schools could strain resources and reduce education quality through larger class sizes. In the model, state school quality is assumed to be fixed and unaffected by enrolment. The videos also highlight that some families may face constraints in switching schools, for example due to special educational needs or strong preferences for particular types of schooling, which are not represented in a simple, representative household framework. In addition, the videos discuss political and fairness considerations behind the policy, which are outside the scope of the model.

To incorporate these arguments, the model could be extended by allowing state school quality to depend on enrolment or public spending. Introducing heterogeneous households with different constraints or preferences would also capture uneven responses to the policy. Finally, explicitly modelling government use of VAT revenues could help analyse potential improvements in state education alongside the costs imposed on private school users.
