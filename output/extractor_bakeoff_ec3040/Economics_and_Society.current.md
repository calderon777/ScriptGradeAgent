The AI summary seems highly accurate regarding the use of economic principles and estimates.
Claim 1: On 1 January 2025, the UK removed VAT exemptions for private schools, applying a 20%
tax to tuition, followed by the removal of 80% business rates relief in April 2025.
Verification: The government website confirms that the “20% VAT rate will be added to private
school fees from 1 January 2025” and charitable private schools would “lose charitable business
rates relief – which provides an 80% discount on the rates… from April 2025.” (GOV.UK, 2024).
Claim 2: To prevent tax avoidance through early payments, the government used anti-forestalling
measure which applied 20% VAT to any fees paid after 29 July 2024 for terms starting in 2025.
Verification: This claim is accurate as the government states that “Any fees paid from 29 July 2024
relating to the term starting in January 2025 and onwards will be subject to VAT.” (GOV.UK, 2024).
Claim 3: Primary benefit of the policy is that it is estimated to raise £1.3 to £1.5 billion a year in
public revenue, which will improve facilities for 94% of children attending public school and recruit
approximately 6500 new teachers.
Verification: This aligns with the IFS reports’s conclusion that “removing tax exemptions would
probably have a net gain to the public finances of about £1.3–£1.5 billion per year” (IFS, 2023, p.
[27]). The revenue generated will “allow for about a 2% increase in day-to-day spending on state
schools” (IFS, 2023, p. [28]). The increase in funding will “enable it to hire 6,500 teachers for public
schools.” (Wang, 2025, p. [228]), which improves the education of approximately 94% students in
state schools since the proportion of students in private school “has remained around 6–7% for at
least the last 20 years” (IFS, 2023, p. [3]).
Claim 4: The government's aim is to reduce educational inequalities and increase contribution of
independent sectors to public infrastructure.
Verification: The IFS report supports the claim, as it states that the policy is intended to “increase
state school spending and would be targeted at pupils from disadvantaged backgrounds” and also
acknowledges the massive spending gap between private and state schools, stating “[the] average
private school fees …[are] nearly 90% higher than state school spending per pupil” (IFS, 2023, p.
[3]). However, the IFS also mention that “If the aim is to… reduce inequalities by school attended,
then this policy package is likely to have only minor impacts.” because the report expects “that the
change in private school attendance levels will be small” (IFS, 2023, p. [28]).
Claim 5: Schools will be able to reclaim VAT on business expenses (such as capital projects and
supplies), which will partially offset the policy’s financial impacts on schools.
Verification: The IFS report supports the claim and states that “The VAT paid by firms is calculated
as the difference between the VAT charged to customers (which would become 20%) and any VAT
paid on inputs… [this] ensures that VAT is not levied multiple times through the supply chain.”
(IFS,2023, p. [16]). The report calculates that when the input deductions are factored in, the policy
“results from an effective VAT rate of 15%” (IFS,2023, p. [3]).
Claim 6: The policy will have negative impacts on independent sectors and families, as private
schools increased fees by 10 to 15% to remain viable, creating significant financial burden for
middle income families and on students with special educational needs but lack formal education,
health, and care plan.
Verification: The IFS report states that “Imposing VAT on private school fees will… [likely] lower
demand for private schooling due to higher posttax prices.” (IFS, 2023, p. [18]), with the
government predicting “to see fees rise by around 10%.” (GOV.UK, 2024).
However, the government also states that “parents and guardians [that] choose to send their child
with SEND to a private school, VAT will be applied to their fees.”, especially those that lack formal
education, health, and care plan (EHCP) (GOV.UK, 2024).
This places a financial burden on middle income families (especially those with SEND children but
lack EHCP) that prefer to send their child to private school, as increasing the cost of private school
will add more to the borrowing constraints and becomes even more unaffordable, forcing them to
send their children to state schools instead. (lecture 9 slide 8; slide 11)
Claim 7: It is estimated that approximately 35000 to 37000 pupils may migrate to the state sector,
which could cause overcrowding and capacity issues in already oversubscribed local authorities.
Verification: The government expects that “around 35,000 pupils… across the UK will change
schools to a state school from private schools.” (GOV.UK, 2024), which could cause an even bigger
capacity issue in an “already overcrowded public system” (Ye, 2025, p. [202]).

2.6
To assess whether introduction of VAT on private schools is a good idea, the trade-off between the
redistributive equity and risk of systematic inefficiency must be evaluated.
The VAT policy aims to reduce inequalities by narrowing the spending gap between the public and
private sector (IFS, 2023, p. [3]), which has been increasing over the years (lecture 9, slide 19).
Increasing spending in state schools will have a greater marginal benefit than the marginal cost
from the private school minorities as derived in Part 2.5. The low income families in the state sector
will benefit from the VAT redistribution for state schools, as it will increase teacher recruitments
and improve infrastructure for the 94% of students in state schools (Wang, 2025, p. [228]; IFS,
2023, p. [3]).
However, the policy creates a burden for middle income families that prefer to send their child to
private school but are forced out into the state sector because the VAT made the tuition

Possible reasons why endogeneity might occur:
Reverse causality: Although tuition fees may drive school quality, the reverse could also be true.
High quality schools have higher demand, which allows them to charge higher tuition. This makes it
harder to distinguish whether are an input for quality or the cause of it, which biases the
coefficient.
Confounding variables: High income families can afford expensive tuition whilst also providing
better academic support at home. This means that schools with higher fees may show better
results due to the students’ unobserved backgrounds rather than the school’s investment in
resources. This causes an omitted variable bias problem.
A solution to the endogeneity issue could be solved by conducting a randomized control trial
experiment, by randomly allocating students to schools with different tuition levels.

of their family income or ability, it means that parents don’t choose which schools to send their
children to, breaking the link between parental wealth and tuition fees. It also ensures that the
tuition fees are not correlated with any unobserved factors in the error term (𝜀 ), which isolates the
