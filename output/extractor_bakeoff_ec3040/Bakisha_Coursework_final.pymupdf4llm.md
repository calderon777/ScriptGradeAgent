The proposal to apply Value Added Tax (VAT) to private school fees in the UK would remove the VAT exemption and apply the standard 20% rate to tuition fees. Private schools charge fees, but benefit from favourable tax treatment despite education being a private, excludable good (Topic 9, p.7). The IFS summarises the reform as Labour plans to “levy VAT at the main rate of 20% on private school fees” (IFS report 2023, p.5). This would increase the price of private schooling overall. 

A key objective of the policy is to raise government revenue while improving fairness in the tax system. The IFS estimates that removing tax exemptions from private schools would raise around £1.6 billion a year in extra tax revenue (IFS report 2023, p.3), But it stresses that there is “still lots of uncertainty” because outcomes depend on how schools and parents respond on avoidance risks (IFS report 2023, p.4). After allowing for extra state sector spending needs, the IMF suggests a more cautious net gain of about £1.3- £1.5 billion per year in the long run (IFS report 2023, p.4). Consistent with taxation lectures statutory rates alone do not determine revenue. Price changes affect demand and supply, so revenue depends on elasticities and behavioural responses. If higher fees reduce enrolment or increase bursary provision, the net gains could be smaller. 

Private school attendance is heavily concentrated amongst high income households (Topic 9, p.18). The IFS similarly notes that “most pupils attending private schools come from families at the top of both the income and wealth distribution” (IFS report 2023, p.5). This implies that the 

burden would fall mainly on richer families though some middle-income households may also adjust. 

The IFS highlights that if private attendance falls, “state schools will require extra funding to accommodate them” (IFS report 2023, p.4). This estimates around £100 to £300 million extra spending per year under central assumptions (IFS report 2023, p.4). Overall, the policy trades equity and revenue gains against behaviour adjustments and short run capacity pressures.
