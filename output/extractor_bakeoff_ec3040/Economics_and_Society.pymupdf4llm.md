## 3.4 

The regression for prediction 1 is likely to violate the exogeneity assumption (𝐸(𝜖|𝑡) = 0), meaning the tuition variable is correlated with the error term (𝜖), making  𝛽1 biased and inconsistent. 

Possible reasons why endogeneity might occur: 

Reverse causality: Although tuition fees may drive school quality, the reverse could also be true. High quality schools have higher demand, which allows them to charge higher tuition. This makes it harder to distinguish whether are an input for quality or the cause of it, which biases the coefficient. 

Confounding variables: High income families can afford expensive tuition whilst also providing better academic support at home. This means that schools with higher fees may show better results due to the students’ unobserved backgrounds rather than the school’s investment in resources. This causes an omitted variable bias problem. 

A solution to the endogeneity issue could be solved by conducting a randomized control trial experiment, by randomly allocating students to schools with different tuition levels. 

By randomly assigning the treatment (private school attendance) to groups of students regardless of their family income or ability, it means that parents don’t choose which schools to send their children to, breaking the link between parental wealth and tuition fees. It also ensures that the tuition fees are not correlated with any unobserved factors in the error term (𝜀𝑖), which isolates the true causal effect of school resources on quality. 

However, this type of experiment is very hard to conduct in practice for several reasons, as denying the control group students access to high quality education for the purpose of the experiment is highly unethical. In addition, funding full tuition for thousands of students to attend private schools would also be very expensive. Furthermore, to get statistically significant results, the trial would need to span several years to track academic progression, making the process slow.
