by removing tax privilege for fee-charging schools whilst providing funds to address inequalities

particularly in areas where private school fees are already close to affordability thresholds. IFS

economic perspective, there also may be efficiency gains if wealthier pupils integrate into state
schools. As Lecture 9 (p.15) explains, segregation across schools “perpetuates and amplifies
inequalities” as students from wealthier backgrounds benefit from peer effects. Therefore,
reducing this segregation could create positive effects. However, net impact will depend on
behavioural responses, regional capacity and implementation design.

The regression may suffer from endogeneity because funding per pupil (which links to VAT
revenue) is likely to be influenced by unobserved factors such as school quality or student
composition. These factors both affect public-school outcomes, creating omitted variable bias
and making it difficult to isolate the effect of funding. There may also be reverse causality, as
schools with better outcomes may receive more funding. In addition, the model in part 2
highlights selection effects since an increase in VAT can change which families attend public
schools, which then affects peer quality and student composition.
One way to address these issues is to include control variables, such as class size and
measures of pupil background, which can help reduce bias. Another approach can be to
compare changes in outcomes within the same schools over time, which helps control
unobserved factors that are constant for each school and are difficult to measure directly.
Overall, without addressing these endogeneity issues, it would be difficult to interpret the
estimated effect of VAT-funded resources on public-school outcomes.
