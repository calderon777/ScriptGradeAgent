Rewritten
The proposal to apply Value Added Tax (VAT) to private school fees in the UK would remove the
VAT exemption and apply the standard 20% rate to tuition fees. Private schools charge fees, but
benefit from favourable tax treatment despite education being a private, excludable good (Topic
9, p.7). The IFS summarises the reform as Labour plans to “levy VAT at the main rate of 20% on
private school fees” (IFS report 2023, p.5). This would increase the price of private schooling
overall.
A key objective of the policy is to raise government revenue while improving fairness in the tax
system. The IFS estimates that removing tax exemptions from private schools would raise
around £1.6 billion a year in extra tax revenue (IFS report 2023, p.3), But it stresses that there is
“still lots of uncertainty” because outcomes depend on how schools and parents respond on
avoidance risks (IFS report 2023, p.4). After allowing for extra state sector spending needs, the
IMF suggests a more cautious net gain of about £1.3- £1.5 billion per year in the long run (IFS
report 2023, p.4). Consistent with taxation lectures statutory rates alone do not determine
revenue. Price changes affect demand and supply, so revenue depends on elasticities and
behavioural responses. If higher fees reduce enrolment or increase bursary provision, the net
gains could be smaller.
Private school attendance is heavily concentrated amongst high income households (Topic 9,
p.18). The IFS similarly notes that “most pupils attending private schools come from families at
the top of both the income and wealth distribution” (IFS report 2023, p.5). This implies that the

burden would fall mainly on richer families though some middle-income households may also
adjust.
The IFS highlights that if private attendance falls, “state schools will require extra funding to
accommodate them” (IFS report 2023, p.4). This estimates around £100 to £300 million extra
spending per year under central assumptions (IFS report 2023, p.4). Overall, the policy trades
equity and revenue gains against behaviour adjustments and short run capacity pressures.

Part 4
The theoretical model in Part 2, with the empirical approach in Part 3, helps evaluate the
arguments in the videos “VAT on private school is a jealousy tax” and “Why Labour’s private
school VAT policy is unfair”. Both oppose VAT on private-school fees, but stress different
concerns.
The model refers to the distributional concerns in “VAT on private schools is a jealousy tax”. The
video argues that the policy unfairly targets wealthier families and reflects resentment than
economic reasoning. The model partly supports this view as it shows that high income families
are more likely to continue sending their children to private schools and therefore pay most of
the VAT. However, the model highlights an important counterpoint: VAT revenue raises the payoff
in public schools which benefits families to stay in the public schools particularly lower income
households. By holding school attendance fixed, Part 2 shows a rise in VAT can increase overall
social welfare. Part 3 looks at the changes in private-school attendance across income groups
and helps determine whether middle-income families are most likely to switch.

“Why Labour’s private school is unfair” focuses more on practical concerns as pressures on
public-schools and families being pushed out of private education. Although, the model allows
for switching when costs rise, it involves supply constraints and non-price factors which
determine whether public schools are suitable for children with special educational needs.
Public schooling is treated as one homogeneous with identical provisions for all families, so
these concerns are not fully captured.
To address this, the model could be extended with preferences beyond income, including
capacity limits and quality differences across public schools, and incorporates broader views
about fairness and redistribution. These extensions would make the model better suited to
analysing the real-world concerns, providing more realistic evaluation on private-school fees.
