5)

Increasing the value-added tax (VAT) on private school fees can increase overall social welfare if school choices remain unchanged. This is because it transfers resources from private families to the public school system in a way that benefits more people. Families attending private schools pay more out-of-pocket, making them less fortunate on an individual basis, but the VAT they pay is combined and used to boost public school funding. Because all students in public schools receive this additional funding, the overall benefit to public school families may outweigh the overall loss to private school families. In this instance, VAT serves as a tool for redistribution, moving funds from households with generally higher incomes to better public education. However, behaviour determines this beneficial welfare effect.

The welfare advantage may be reduced or even eliminated if a higher VAT causes many families to transfer their children from private to public schools, making the public system more congested and costly to operate. If VAT is evaded or is expensive to collect, welfare may also decline. In summary, VAT can lower welfare when behavioural and administrative costs are high, but it can raise welfare when reactions are low and revenue is used efficiently.

6)

In theory, the imposition of VAT on private school fees is justified, but it is risky. Our theoretical model illustrates the immediate effect. While the tax revenue can be combined to increase funding for public schools, adding VAT increases the amount of money families must pay for private education, which lowers private families' disposable income. The redistribution can raise overall welfare if school choices do not significantly change because public gains (spread among numerous students) may exceed private losses. This is consistent with the lecture discussion that public funding can address inequality and capture wider social returns to education (lecture 9, 2023, p.8).

Evidence, however, alerts us to trade-offs. VAT raises the cost of private education and may force marginal (usually middle-class) families into public schools, increasing crowding, potentially lowering average quality in both sectors through composition effects, and raising marginal public costs. Peer effects and the concentration of wealthier students in private schools are highlighted in empirical slides, therefore, fee taxation is progressive but may result in disruptive changes in enrolment and outcomes (lecture 9, 2023, pp.14-15, 21-24).

It's evident who wins and who loses. Students attending public schools, many of whom have lower incomes, benefit from increased funding and possibly less segregation. Families who pay more for private schools are the losers, and if enrolment declines, private quality may suffer. The wealthiest households are least impacted because nonfinancial factors (status, networks) keep them enrolled in private schools, while middleclass households are most vulnerable to being pushed across the divide.

Designing policies is essential with reforms that could include a modest VAT with robust anti-avoidance measures, targeted assistance for underprivileged students , and phased implementation to allow public capacity to grow to generate revenue while minimising harm. To maintain choice while lowering inequality, lecture materials emphasise the importance of complementarities between public funding and targeted policy (lecture 9, 2023, p.15). In general, VAT on private fees can be included in a package of fair policies, but only if it is applied carefully and in conjunction with safeguards.

This model may suffer from endogeneity issues for a few reasons listed below.

- Omitted variables: Private enrolment and VAT policy discussions may be impacted by unobserved factors like shifts in public school reforms or attitudes towards private education. 𝑉𝐴𝑇𝑡 will be correlated with the error term if these are not fully captured by controls.

- Policy endogeneity (reverse causality): Due to the high or increasing use of private schools, the government may implement VAT. Prices and preferences determine private enrolment in the model and if enrolment trends impact policy, causality is reciprocal.

- General equilibrium effects: VAT has an impact on public funding and crowding, as was demonstrated in previous sections. The interpretation of 𝛽 may be complicated by the indirect influence of these feedback effects on school choices.

Potential fix:

Difference-in-differences (DiD): Examine how private enrolment changed before and after the implementation of the VAT in comparison to a control group (such as age groups or less exposed regions). This is practical and takes advantage of the policy's timing.

The videos highlight significant, pressing issues: VAT raises the cost of private education, which drives middle-class families out of the market and puts more strain on public schools (Video 1). It may be viewed as a "jealousy tax" that damages private schools and puts them at risk of closure (Video 2). The fundamental price-driven mechanism is captured by our theoretical model as higher VAT increases the out-ofpocket expense of private education and, ceteris paribus, decreases private enrolment. The model also accounts for distributional effects as middle-class families are more likely to transfer to public schools, while wealthier households with stronger non-price preferences stay private. This directly relates to the main concerns raised in the videos regarding middle-class switching and overcrowding in public schools.

Nevertheless, the model does not include a few of the arguments in the videos. In contrast to the three-family model, the speakers emphasise system capacity (teacher shortages, strikes), changing supply responses (private school closures), macro spillovers (lower household spending), and behavioural responses. The model should be expanded to include households across income percentiles (rather than just three types), permit private schools to modify capacity and price, add a public-school capacity constraint and per-pupil marginal cost, and model dynamic responses in order to analyse these. These changes enable us to examine crowding, closure risk, and short versus long-term impacts on costs and quality.

The ISC Annual Census (school level pupil counts), DfE data (total pupils by area to form private shares), and HMRC records for VAT timing can all be used to empirically test the key prediction, “↑ 𝜏(VAT rate) → ↑ private enrolment.” It is appropriate to compare pre/post trends in private shares across regions using a difference in differences or event study design that accounts for cohort size and demographics. Subgroup analysis by local income to see if middle classes respond more and looking for school closures in ISC panels are helpful robustness checks.

The main limitations still exist because policy implementation might coincide with other shocks, avoidance or fee adjustments might conceal actual effects, and closure dynamics need in-depth school studies. Use external tools or staggered implementation whenever possible and provide event plots and heterogeneity checks.

Overall, the model explains the videos price and distribution, but evaluating capacity, closures, and broader macro effects needs model extensions and more targeted empirical work.
