4) Edited summary

The policy would increase state school spending by applying VAT to private school fees (removing existing exemptions) and exempting specialist placements funded by local authorities. Before behavioural effects, the Institute

for Fiscal Studies estimates an additional £1.6 billion in tax revenues (IFS, 2023, p.3; Table 2, p.15). Even though VAT is a regressive consumption tax, allocating those revenues to support underprivileged students is likely to result in a progressive outcome because private education is concentrated among wealthier households (IFS, 2023, p.3; Lecture 2, slide 26). Therefore, the main advantages are evident: an increase in fiscal revenue and the possibility of redistribution through higher public education spending aimed at those in need.

If the legal definition of private schooling is not strictly enforced, the main costs include higher out-of-pocket expenses for families who continue to use private provision, increased administrative complexity for regulators and schools, and opportunities for tax evasion (IFS, 2023, p.17-19). A 15% effective VAT could reduce private attendance over the medium to long term by about 3-7 percent (roughly 20,000-40,000 pupils), which would in turn create costs for the state sector. However, the IFS judges that short run demand is relatively inelastic, so immediate falls in private attendance are likely to be small (IFS, 2023, p.21-24).

According to the report's main assumptions, the package still results in a net public finance gain even though the additional state cost is small, roughly £100300 million per year (IFS, 2023, p. 24-25).
