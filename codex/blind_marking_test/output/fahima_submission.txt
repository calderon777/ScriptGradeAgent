1.2 Comments on accuracy  
The AI-generated output provides a reasonable overview of the proposed VAT reform, but while 
many of its claims are directionally correct, several are either incomplete or overstated when 
assessed against the IFS report and course material. 

 
 
The description of the policy itself is accurate. The AI correctly states that private school fees are 
currently exempt from VAT and that the reform would remove this exemption. This is directly 
supported by the IFS, which states that “independent school fees are currently VAT exempt” and 
that Labour proposes levying VAT on these fees (IFS, Executive Summary, p.3). As a factual 
description of the policy change, the output is therefore dependable. 
However, the AI output becomes less accurate when discussing the size of the price increase faced 
by households. By referring to VAT at the standard rate without further qualification, it implicitly 
suggests a 20% rise in fees. The IFS explicitly cautions against this interpretation, noting that “the 
effective VAT rate would be around 15% rather than the full 20%” once input deductions and 
exemptions are considered (IFS, Executive Summary, p.3; Table 2, pp.16-17). This distinction matters 
economically, as overstating the price increase can lead to exaggerated predictions about demand 
responses. 
The claim that the policy would raise substantial government revenue is supported by the evidence. 
The IFS estimates gross revenues of around £1.6 billion per year, with a net gain of £1.3-£1.5 billion 
after allowing for additional state school spending (IFS, pp.24-25). From a public finance perspective, 
this suggests the revenue argument for the policy is strong and credible. (Mirrlees et al., 2011). 
Where the AI output is weakest is in its treatment of behavioural effects. It suggests that higher fees 
could trigger widespread school closures and major disruption. The IFS evidence does not support 
this. Based on observed behaviour and elasticity estimates, the report suggests a 3-7% fall in private 
school attendance, equivalent to 20,000–40,000 pupils, occurring gradually over time (IFS, pp.21-
22). The IFS also notes that demand for private schooling appears price-inelastic, implying that many 
families will absorb higher costs rather than exit the sector, making “large-scale closures unlikely in 
the short run” (IFS, pp.21-23). 
In the course's terms, the policy debate is best framed as an equity–efficiency trade-off: removing a 
VAT exemption targets a service disproportionately consumed by higher-income households, while 
raising funds for a publicly provided service with large distributional implications. This links directly 
to the course discussion of why public and private schooling co-exist and how a two-tier system can 
amplify inequality even when ‘choice’ is given. 
Overall, while the AI output captures the broad trade-offs involved in the policy, it lacks the nuance 
provided by empirical evidence. Applying concepts from the course, particularly tax incidence and 
elasticity of demand, suggests that the policy is more likely to raise revenue with limited behavioural 
disruption than the AI output implies. (Atkinson and Stiglitz, 1980; Mirrlees et al., 2011).

improve tax fairness, and generate limited disruption to the education system. From an economic 
perspective, this suggests that much of the burden of the tax is likely to be borne by higher-income 
households rather than translated into large behavioural responses.

A potential drawback arises if VAT is set too high. Large increases in VAT may induce middle-income 
families to switch from private to public schooling, increasing congestion and reducing per-pupil 
resources in public schools. In this case, the efficiency costs of overcrowding may offset the 
redistributive benefits. This equity efficiency trade-off shows the broader policy evaluation 
framework, which was mentioned in Lecture 3, where redistributive gains must be weighed against 
potential efficiency losses arising from congestion and capacity restraints. 
Overall, the model suggests that VAT on private schools can improve both equity and social welfare 
if implemented moderately and if the additional revenue is used effectively. However, excessively 
high VAT rates risk undermining efficiency by overburdening the public education system. The model 
supports VAT on private schooling as a potentially effective redistributive tool, but only when 
applied cautiously and alongside sufficient public-sector capacity.

3.4 [7.5 marks] 
 
Even with fixed effects, the regression may suffer from endogeneity for several reasons. This reflects 
the module’s wider point that education outcomes are hard to interpret causally because private-
school attendance is strongly selected on family characteristics and estimated ‘private school effects’ 
fall once income and parental education are controlled for. 
i) Omitted variables correlated with exposure and trends 
Areas with high baseline private-school shares may also differ in unobserved ways that evolve over 
time (e.g., local housing market dynamics, changing school quality, peer effects). In the theoretical 
model, these map into shifts in effective valuations 𝑃𝑖 and in the attractiveness of public schooling, 
potentially making 𝜀𝑟𝑡 correlated with 𝐸𝑥𝑝𝑜𝑠𝑢𝑟𝑒𝑟 × 𝑃𝑜𝑠𝑡𝑡. 
ii) Simultaneity in fee-setting and demand. Although the Part 2 model treats 𝑡 as a parameter, 
private schools may adjust fees in response to demand. High demand (unobserved parental 
preferences) can raise both fees and enrolment, biasing price-based estimates if one uses 𝑡(1 + 𝜏) 
directly as a regressor. 
iii) Policy endogeneity and concurrent reforms 
The timing of VAT could coincide with other national policy changes affecting schooling choices 
(public school funding reforms, bursary policies). If these reforms are correlated with 𝑃𝑜𝑠𝑡𝑡, the 
estimated effect may conflate VAT with other changes. 
 
Solutions and feasibility 
Difference-in-differences with robustness checks 
The exposure based DiD framework above is a practical way to mitigate simultaneity in fees because 
it relies on a policy discontinuity and pre-determined exposure rather than endogenous 

 
 
contemporaneous fees. To support credibility, one would evaluate parallel pre-trends in private-
school shares between high- and low-exposure areas. 
Instrumental variables (IV) for price-based regressions 
If the empirical approach uses the VAT-inclusive fee 𝑡(1 + 𝜏) directly, variation in 𝜏 induced by policy 
can serve as an instrument for the VAT-inclusive price component (conceptually consistent with the 
model where 𝜏 is policy-driven. The practicality depends on having sufficient variation and a strong 
first stage. 
Augmenting controls and using outcomes data  
Incorporating time-varying controls (income, demographics) and proxies for public-school quality 
(class size indicators, inspection outcomes) can reduce omitted variable bias.  
The main threats are omitted variables, fee endogeneity, and contemporaneous reforms. A policy-
based DiD strategy with exposure, supported by pre-trend checks and rich controls, is the most 
implementable approach in practice, with IV as a complementary strategy for price-based 
specifications. (Angrist and Pischke, 2009). These concerns highlight why policy-driven variation, 
rather than cross-sectional price difference, is the most credible way to assess the models’ 
predictions. This reflects a broader theme discussed in the lectures, namely that policy evaluation 
must distinguish price effects from selection effects driven by underlying social and income 
heterogeneity.  
 
Part 4 [15 marks] 
 
The theoretical model developed in Part 2 provides a useful framework for evaluating several of the 
arguments raised in the two YouTube videos discussing the introduction of VAT on private school 
fees. In particular, the model clarifies the economic mechanisms linking higher private schooling 
costs, public school funding, and school choice behaviour. 
In the course framing, the arguments in the videos can be organised around an equity–efficiency 
trade-off: who bears the burden of the tax and who benefits from the implied change in public 
funding, versus whether the policy creates distortions through switching and pressure on capacity. 
A central claim made in the videos is that applying VAT to private school fees will increase the 
financial burden on families using private education and may push some pupils into the state sector. 
This mechanism is directly captured in the model through the VAT-inclusive price. 𝑡(1 + 𝜏), which 
lowers the utility of private schooling as the VAT rate increases. The model predicts that middle-
income families are most likely to switch from private to public schooling, since they are closest to 
indifference between the two sectors. This aligns closely with concerns in the videos about families 
“at the margin” being disproportionately affected. The empirical strategy outlined in Part 3 allows 
this prediction to be assessed by examining whether private-school enrolment falls more sharply in 
areas with greater exposure to private education following the VAT reform. THIS shows a contrast 
between theoretical mechanisms and real-world complexity reflects the course’s broader emphasis, 
which is a good example of the course’s broader emphasis on linking a positive mechanism (how 
incentives change) to an empirical strategy (what we would observe in data), before moving to 
normative claims about whether the policy is ‘good’. 

 
 
The videos also argue that VAT revenue could improve state school funding but may be insufficient 
to offset the pressure created by increased demand. This trade-off is reflected in the public-school 
payoff term. 𝑆/𝑥 + 5𝜏𝑡𝑦/𝑥, which shows that funding gains from VAT are shared among public-
school pupils and therefore depend on enrolment. However, the model abstracts from short-run 
capacity constraints, teacher shortages, and adjustment costs, all of which are emphasised in the 
videos but not explicitly modelled. (Sibieta, 2025). This matters because, as discussed in the 
education lecture, state-school running costs are funded from general taxation (government 
budget), so the policy effectively shifts part of education financing from general revenues towards a 
tax concentrated on private-school users. This mirrors broader debates on state capacity, where the 
effectiveness of the redistributive policy depends not only on revenue raised but also on institutional 
ability to absorb increased demands.  
Several arguments raised in the videos, therefore, fall outside the scope of the current framework. 
These include transitional disruption effects, heterogeneity in public-school quality, and broader 
social considerations such as peer effects. (Hoxby, 2000). To analyse these issues, the model could 
be extended by allowing public-school quality to depend non-linearly on enrolment, or by 
introducing capacity constraints in the public sector. Another extension would be to introduce 
dynamic adjustment costs, distinguishing short-run disruptions from long-run equilibria. (Hoxby, 
2000). 
What I find most persuasive in the videos is the focus on the ‘squeezed middle’ response: the model 
makes it clear that the largest behavioural movement should come from families close to 
indifference, not from the very highest-income households. Overall, the model provides a disciplined 
way to interpret the main economic arguments raised in the videos, while also highlighting which 
elasticity of concerns require additional modelling beyond the current framework.
