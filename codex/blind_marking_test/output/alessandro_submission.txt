Prompt: I am interested in the recent policy proposal to introduce VAT on private school fees. Generate a 300 word summary of the VAT policy. The summary should cover the main changes introduced by the policy and its likely costs and benefits.
Response: The UK government has introduced a policy to remove the long-standing VAT exemption on private school fees, meaning that from January 2025 most education and boarding fees charged by independent schools are subject to the standard 20 per cent rate of Value Added Tax. Previously, private education was treated as a VAT-exempt service, reflecting its charitable status and historical precedent. Under the new policy, private schools must register for VAT if their turnover exceeds the normal threshold and account for VAT on fees paid for education and closely related services. State schools and nurseries remain unaffected, and where local authorities place pupils in private schools, particularly those with special educational needs that cannot be met in the state sector, the authorities can reclaim the VAT incurred.
The government expects the policy to generate significant additional revenue, estimated at around £460 million in its first year and rising to over £1.5 billion annually in the longer term. This revenue is intended to support public services, particularly increased investment in state education. However, the policy is also expected to increase costs for families using private schools. While some schools may absorb part of the VAT by reducing margins, many are likely to pass on at least some of the cost to parents through higher fees. Average fee increases are expected to be around 10 per cent, though the impact will vary between schools. Models project about 37,000 fewer pupils in the private sector in the long run, equating to around 6 % of pupils moving to state schools or alternative provision. As a result, placing additional pressure on already stretched state school resources.
Supporters argue that the policy promotes fairness by ending a tax relief that primarily benefits wealthier households and by redirecting funds toward improving education for the majority of children educated in the state system. Critics, however, warn that it may reduce parental choice, threaten the viability of smaller or specialist private schools, and create unintended consequences for pupils with specific educational or faith-based needs. Overall, the policy represents a significant shift in the taxation of education, with clear fiscal benefits but potentially complex social and educational impacts.
Overall, the statement generated in question 1 proves to be largely accurate and a reliable summary of the policy to introduce VAT on private school fees, and it closely aligns with the analysis of the IFS report. As a result, this policy has the potential to be desirable if the revenue it generates is used efficiently and with purpose, towards improving public schooling. Without this calculated investment it risks creating larger efficiency losses than it does equity gains.

The main coefficient for this prediction to test the theory is β3. β3 measures how VAT affects private school attendance for high-income families. β1 is the baseline effect of VAT on low-income families. So, the total VAT effect for high-income families is β1 + β3.
Hypothesis test would be to check β3 for its value and significance (done using t-test for coefficient) :
H0 : β3 = 0 (the effect of VAT is independent of family income status)
H1 : β3 ≠̂ 0 (the effect of VAT depends on family income status)
If our prediction is correct and H1 is true, if β3 >0, high-income families reduce private school attendance by less than low-income families. If β3 <0, high-income families respond more strongly to VAT effect.
The regression used in question 3 can suffer from multiple endogeneity issues, meaning that the estimated effect of VAT on private school attendance may be biased and not reflect a true relationship.
The number one concern is omitted variable bias, unobserved factors that affect the VAT policy and the schooling choices of parents can play a large role. This can be variables such as parental preferences, cultural differences/attitudes, schooling expectations… which can influence private school attendance but are not captured in our model. This represents the unobserved deeper components of PM, PL, and PH that affect the utility of each family with respect to private schooling.
To minimize omitted variable bias and obtain as accurate correlation as possible, we can include control variables into the model that can account for several of these factors such as; parental education, regional effects, race… However, preferences are difficult to observe therefore this approach is limited in the feedback it can produce.
Another concern is reverse causality. Government decisions about VAT policy may be due to changes in private school attendance or inequality and demand for education. Therefore, private school attendance may not decrease due to the implementation of VAT on fees, but VAT was implemented due to an already declining private school attendance in order to subsidise the increased public-school attendance and reduce inequality of education.
Additionally, there may be a case of measurement error. Some families may have responded to the introduction of VAT and moved their children form private school to public school before policy was implemented due to the early announcement. This would be missed from our model and would be difficult to account for. A method to counteract this could be to observe changes in private school attendance for high-income families and low-income families the year prior to introduction of VAT and compare with effects from after policy is introduced.
Overall, our regression faces endogeneity from omitted variable bias, reverse causality, and measurement errors. While the regression could be updated to try and account for these concerns, this will prove difficult to successfully implement and produce reliable and accurate results. Quasi-experiment methods that take into account policy timing are most likely to be implemented practically.
Part 4.
The two videos present contrasting arguments about the introduction of VAT on private school fees. We can analyse these arguments using our theoretical model in part 2 and our predictions prom part 3.
The first video emphasises the capacity constraints of public school and the pressure it faces due to the transition caused by the policy. It argues that the higher private school costs will force families; specifically middle-income;  to cut back on consumption due to lower disposable income or even move their children into already overcrowded public schools which will put even more strain on teachers and create a lower quality of teaching. This can be observed in our model, as the VAT rate increasing from 0-20% has the effect of increasing public school attendance. However, in our model the quality of education received depends on the VAT revenue re-distributed to the public sector per pupil. This fails to acknowledge the overcrowding effects or teacher shortages it may cause, as a result an increase in VAT revenue per pupil may only reflect a maintenance or even decrease level of education instead of an improvement in the level of education in public school as intended.
Alternately, in the second video the broadcaster expresses his encouragement for this policy due to its effects on reducing inequality. The caller argues this tax is a ‘jealousy’ tax suggesting it punishes families who put their children through private education and in term produce a better outcome in the economy later. This is not exactly the case as can be seen in our model because high-income families are less responsive to VAT and unlikely to affect their choice of education ultimately leaving them in the same position they were in before, but it also allows the more disadvantaged or ‘ordinary’ pupils to experience improved quality of teaching and result in a positive labour market outcome. Redistributing VAT revenue towards public education can raise welfare for lower-income families, consistent with the broadcaster’s argument that spreading a little quality more evenly can encourage overall participation and productivity.
Unfortunately, our model does not implement labour supply incentives or social norms such as welfare discouraging young people to work because they will be taken care of financially regardless, or the declining birth rate resulting in lower attendance rates.
Overall, our model from part 2 and data from part 3 provided a strong framework for analysing the effects of the policy and sparked discussions analysing similar trade-offs as discussed in the videos. However, there are lots of intricacies that require much more complex models in order to capture more meaningful results using all of the constraints mentioned.
