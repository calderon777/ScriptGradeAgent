My summary: 
 
The Labour Party proposed to remove the current VAT exemption on private school fees and 
to apply the standard 20% rate. It is confirmed by the IFS, which states that “Labour has 
proposed levying VAT at the main rate of 20% on private school fees” (IFS, 2023, p. 4). The 
main fiscal motivation is to raise revenue for state education. In the short run, behavioural 
responses are expected to be limited, and IFS estimates “£1.5–1.6 billion, representing the 
most likely net public finance impact in the short run” (IFS, 2023, p. 24). These additional 
funds would go toward the state sector, where spending pressures are increased. 
The policy might then affect household schooling decisions. The IFS states: a "3% to 7% fall 
in private school attendance (20,000-40,000 pupils)" can be expected in response to the 
effective VAT rate (IFS, 2023, p. 26), which gives more precise evidence. The resulting 
behavioural effect, though modest, may come with distributional consequences because rich 
households are less responsive to higher prices. 
  
The IFS explains that "current tax exemptions could be seen as playing a similar role to such 
a voucher" and that there are "equity arguments for the state contributing to the cost of 

educating pupils across all sectors" (IFS, 2023, p. 6). Therefore, eliminating the exemption 
improves redistributive goals and enhances the tax system’s neutrality. Lecture 9 proves this 
by showing that state education generates equity and efficiency benefits through externalities, 
borrowing constraints, and peer effects. 
There are also long-run effects on school quality and the role of inelastic demand that are 
worth talking about. The IFS also mentions that private schooling is "likely to be inelastic" 
and that, according to the Ramsey rule, such goods may efficiently be subject to higher 
taxation. Overall, the policy seems financially robust and driven by equitable considerations, 
though its wider educational effects rely on behavioural responses.

2.6 Is VAT on private schools a good idea?  
The desirability of the VAT, therefore, depends on the parameters of the model and how 
families respond to it. On the one hand, VAT could enhance equity and welfare effects 
because of inelastic demand for private education enrolment, as well as effective 
redistribution of VAT revenues generated, accruing benefits for public schooling. In the 
setup, VAT-generated revenues boost welfare from public schooling, as captured through 
5T/x, benefiting those pupils in government schools because of better resources due to 
increased utility from public schooling. Because of much larger enrolment in public 
schooling, such redistribution would be beneficial for many pupils and help in making them 
more equally literate. 
On the other hand, welfare gains from VAT will be diminished – or even negative – if VAT 
causes significant movement of pupils from private to public schools. When many families 
switch, the number of pupils in public schools rises, which reduces public-school utility 
through congestion because baseline quality and funding are spread over more pupils. In this 
case, congestion effects can dominate the gains from additional funding. Welfare effects are 
therefore ambiguous and depend on the elasticity of demand for private schooling, the 
strength of redistribution, and the capacity of the public education system. Consequently, 
VAT is not universally desirable, but its effectiveness depends critically on these underlying 
parameters.

about the results of VAT introduction on private school fees.  
 
Prediction 1: VAT and private school attendance 
 


Raising the VAT rate on private school fees (t (1+ τ)) leads to a lower number and share of 
pupils in private schools. Higher prices of private schooling due to a higher VAT rate have a 
major effect on people who value private schooling least (low and middle-income families). 
 
Prediction 2: Public school funding and private school attendance 
 
An increase in public-school spending per student will lead to an increase in the advantage of 
public education and a subsequent reduction in private-school enrolment. The above can be 
represented in the model by an increase in S or an increase in VAT revenue per public-school 
student given by T/x. 
 
Prediction 3: Income and school choice 
 
Regions in which household income or wealth is high (Pi is high) have more pupils in private 
schools. Wealthy people value private education more and are not very sensitive to prices. 
 
Prediction 4: Diverse reactions to VAT 
 
The negative impact of VAT on private school enrollment is less in the case of high-income 
regions. This is because the demand for private education has less elasticity in the case of 
high-income classes, and the lower classes have a greater tendency to shift to public schools.

3.4 Endogeneity concerns and solutions  
 
Endogeneity is a key challenge in estimating the above regression 
 
Reverse causality: 
 
Going to a private school may have implications for government expenditures. For instance, 
if most children are attending private schools, then the government will pay less to fund 
public schools, resulting in biased estimations by OLS. 
 
Omitted Variables: 
 
Unobservable variables, such as the preference of the culture for private education and the 
tradition of schooling, can influence both public and private education. 
 
Simple errors: 
 
With public expenditure per student potentially measured with error, resulting in attenuation 
bias. 
  
Possible solutions: 
 


- Panel data with fixed effects, adjusting for time-invariant variables (regional 
characteristics). 
- Difference-in-differences, increasing funding for public schools or imposing VAT on 
private school fees. 
- Instrumental variables, through external changes in funding formulas or political 
variables that have an influence on the education spending but not on demand for 
private schools.
