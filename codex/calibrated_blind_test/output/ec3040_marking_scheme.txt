Where
• The unit of observation t is the year of the observation.
• The dependent variable yt would be the share of pupils in England attending
public school vs. those attending private schools.
• The independent variable VATt is a dummy variable taking value 1 in the first
year after the VAT increase, i.e., 2025.

• Xt is a set of control variables that could include for example some proxy for

The coefficient of interest is β1. The theory predicts that the introduction of VAT

4. Endogeneity issues arise when the independent variable is systematically correlated
with the error term. This can arise for two reasons: unobserved omitted variables
which are systematically correlated with both the introduction of VAT and public
school attendance and reverse causality, whereby the introduction of VAT is deter-
mined by the level of public school attendance.
The main concerns with this regression is due to unobserved omitted variables.
Besides the control variables listed above, there could be other factors that can affect
the decision to send children to private school that change over time but cannot be
measured. For instance, parents’ taste for private school might be changing year-
on-year (captured by parameters PM, PH and PL in the model) and be correlated
with other political preferences (e.g. preferences for redistribution). This only
creates an endogeneity issue if changes in these factors are systematically correlated
with the introduction of VAT.
For instance, this could the case here because an increase in preferences for re-
distribution across the population might have been what led to the election of the
Labour party, who introduced the VAT. If people’s preferences for redistributions
are correlated with their preferences for public school over private school (i.e., a
lower Pi), then an increase in preferences for redistribution would both lead to the
VAT being more likely to be introduced AND an increase in attendance in public
schools, EVEN IF there were no direct causal effect from the VAT increase to the
attendance decrease.
This does not mean the increase in VAT does not lead to an increase in public
school attendance, but that the effect of the VAT could be overestimated, as some
of the effect could be simply driven by the confounding variables. If changes in
political preferences towards Labour are positively correlated with a change in pref-
erences towards public school, then our estimate of β1 would overestimate the true
treatment effect. Without a measure of the underlying change in preferences, we
cannot identify how much of the change in the proportion of children going to public
school is due to the VAT or a change in taste.
Some possible solutions would be to use time trends from previous years to net
out the change over time, if there is a clear linear trend. To do this, we could
for example run a difference-in-differences specification across months and local
authorities where the treatment group are the months around the introduction of
15

the reform but in the year prior to the reform. This would address the endogeneity
issue by subtracting the underlying long-term change in unobserved preferences for
public school from the change in public school attendance around the time of the
VAT introduction.
Alternatively, we could conduct a difference-in-differences by comparing the share
of public school attendance in Northern Ireland to the share in counties in the Re-
public of Ireland close to the border, since the Republic of Ireland did not introduce
a VAT on private schools. In this case, the treatment group would be counties in
Northern Ireland and the control group would be counties in the Republic of Ire-
land. This would address the endogeneity issue because omitted variables such as
preferences for public school are unlikely to change systematically differently across
each side of the border so subtracting the difference over time in attendance in the
Republic of Ireland would net out the effect of change in unobservable preferences.
