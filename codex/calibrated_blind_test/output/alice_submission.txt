From January 2025, private school tuition fees which were previously exempt from VAT, became subject to the standard VAT rate, increasing the cost of private education for families. The stated aim of the policy is to raise revenue to fund public schools and promote greater fairness within the education system, but it has generated substantial debate about its distribution, efficient and welfare effects.

The introduction of VAT on private school fees represents a significant shift in how the UK tax system treats education services. Under the reform, private schools lose their long-standing VAT exemption and are required to charge VAT at the standard rate on tuition fees. While this clearly raises the headline price of private education, the Institute for Fiscal Studies (IFS) emphasises that schools will be able to reclaim VAT on their inputs, such as maintenance and supplies. As a result, the effective VAT rate is estimated to be closer to 15% rather than the full 20% (IFS, pp.3 and 16). This distinction is crucial for accurately assessing the true price impact faced by families.
The extent to which this VAT increase is passed on to parents depends on the economic incidence of the tax, which in turn determined by the elasticity of demand for private schooling. Consistent with standard tax incidence theory, the IFS finds that demand for private schooling is relatively inelastic in the short run, implying that most of the tax burden is likely to be borne by parents rather than absorbed by schools (IFS, pp.3 and 11). However, overtime behavioural responses are more likely. The IFS estimates a 3-7% reduction in private school attendance in the longer run (IFS, p.26), suggesting that some families may move their children into the state sector. Lecture 9 highlights that private schooling reduces pressure on state school resources (slide 14), implying that any migration back into the public system could increase fiscal and capacity pressures, particularly in already stretched areas.
In terms of equity, the policy is broadly progressive. Private school attendance is heavily concentrated among high-income households (IFS, p.3; Lecture 9 slide 25), so applying VAT effectively shifts part of the tax burden towards families with a greater ability to pay. However, this does not mean the policy is distributionally neutral. Families attending lower-fee independent schools, including some middle-income households, may be disproportionately affected, raising concerns about horizontal equity.
Overall, the VAT reform reflects a trade-off between revenue generation (estimated at £1.3 -1.5 billion per year (IFS, p.26)). This leads to potential costs arising from behavioural responses, increased pressure on state schools, and distribution, consequences. While the policy is well motivated on equity grounds, its effectiveness depends on how both families and schools adjust in practice.

Endogeneity concerns and solutions: The proposed regression may have several endogeneity problems, which could lead to bias the estimated effect of VAT on private school attendance. These issues arise both from omitted variables from the behavioural mechanisms highlighted in the theoretical model.
Firstly, omitted variable bias is a key concern. In the models, the school choices depend not only on VAT but also on public school quality, baseline presences for private schooling and local education conditions. If these factors are correlated with VAT exposure and not fully controlled, the estimated VAT coefficient will be biased. For example, areas with higher0qualiry public schools may also be more politically supportive of redistributive policies such as VAT, leading to a negative relationship between VAT and private school attendance. A solution is to include individual or area fixed effects and time fixed effects, which control for time invariant observed preferences and common shocks. This solution is practical and widely used.
In addition, simultaneity and policy endogeneity may arise. The theoretical model shows that VAT revenue affects public school quality, which in turn affects school choice. If public school quality responds to changes in private school attendance, the direction of causality becomes unclear. In this case VAT is not strictly exogenous it may be introduced in response to perceived inequality or declining public school quality. A solution for this is to use a difference-in-difference design (DiD). This would mean treating the VAT introduction as an exogenous policy shock. This approach relies on the parallel trend assumption, which can be evaluated using pre-reform data. In practice, this is feasible if the reform is implemented nationally at a clearly defined point in time.
Furthermore, selection bias may arise because families with stronger preferences for private education (high Pi) are less responsive to VAT. If these preferences are unobserved, estimated effects may conflate true price sensitivity with selection on unobservable. An instrumental variables (IV) approach, using exogenous variation such as pre reform private school fee levels interacted with VAT, which could address this. Although finding a valid instrument in practice may be challenging.
Overall, while endogeneity concerns are significant, the combination of fixed effects, difference-in-difference, and robustness checks make it possible to credibility evaluate the models’ predictions, provided high quality data is available.
